Summary

CLASH survey provides an unprecedented opportunity to investigate how precisely we can infer stellar properties - ages and metallicities - by fitting theoretical spectra from stellar population syntheses to spatially-resolved spectral energy distributions (SEDs) spanning a very broad wavelength range. Here, we apply this method to study recent star formation in the brightest cluster galaxies (BCG) of a CLASH cluster MACS J0329.7-0211 (hereafter MACS0329) that exhibit spatially extended UV emission which are claimed to be due to star formation (Donahue et al. 2015). It is an example of one of those that reside in dynamically relaxed clusters which preferential exhibit luminous optical line-emitting gas and star formation, implicating cooling of the intracluster gas for the recent star formation. If so, then the star formation in these galaxies should be extended not just over space but also over time, and have a metallicity resembling that of the intracluster gas. Figure. 1 shows that the UV morphology of the BCG of MACS0329 is still visible in the longest wavelength filters.

Modeling the host component

In order to study the young stellar population through its SED, the crucial step is to separate the light from young stellar population and the host galaxy since fitting SED with integrated populations is highly degenerate and under con-strained. The surface brightness profile of old stellar popu-lation in BCG is, fortunately, smooth and symmetrical just like all other elliptical galaxies. Thus, we could create a 2D model that represent the light distribution of the old stellar population and subtract it from each filter images. The re-maining residual images would contain presumably mostly light from young stellar population, and possibly emission line nebula, dust and other cluster members. With a long time tuning the model, we finally obtained a model of the light distribution of old stellar population which consist of three 2D Sersic functions stacked aligning to the center. To determine how well we have subtracted the old stellar population, we carefully inspected the subtracted images to see the consistency across filters and how well they matched the features seen in the color images [Figure.2 (a) &(b)]. Also, in Figure.2 (c), we plotted the radial light profile along the major axis of both the original data and the Sersic model. Lastly, we inspected the SED of the subtracted Ser-sic model and the SED of the original data at a off-UV cen-tral region, overlying with a SDSS spectral templates of “Luminous Red Galaxy”. Note that by not relying on SEDs to model the old stellar population, we overcome the de-generacy of modeling the SEDs of both the younger and older stellar populations simultaneously as performed in previous studies.

Color-color diagrams

With the residual images and the simple stellar popula-tion (SSP) models (Zackrisson et al. 2011,2001), we can easily have a quick and global look of the young stellar pop-ulation by making color-color diagrams using different filter combinations with or without known strong emission lines. With this analysis, we can examine whether the residual images possess colors similar to young star clusters. As seen in Figure 3, the data points clustered around the evo-lution track with some scattering which may be caused by photometric error (indicated by colored error bars) and the fact that light in each pixel may be an integration of more than one star clusters of different age. Moreover, a general picture of the properties of the young stellar population is also revealed by these diagrams. For example, the young stellar population clearly span a range of ages from a few Myr to few tens of Myr. Al-so, the turn at the youngest end of the evolutionary track show hints for nebula emissions. As shown in Figure 3, models with different metallicities (Z) looks similar in over-all trend, indicating that these CC diagrams are not sensi-tive to Z. As a consequence, we are free from the age-Z degeneracy as the data points span roughly the same age range from 20% solar Z to solar Z. Also, the overall distribu-tion of colors does not hint any significant global reddening due to dust extinction. Next, we would like to see if inferring the entire SED would give consistent predictions.

MCMC inference

MCMC inference To interpret the broadband SED of young stellar popula-tion, we adopted an Bayesian approach to constrain the parameters and the uncertainties. The challenges of this task are the degeneracies between parameters including not only the well-known age-metallicity degeneracy, but also the dust extinction-age at host galaxy. We thus applied an algorithm named Nested Sampling which is designed to sample multimodal distributions.
